Srinivasa Ramanujan: The Intuitive Genius of Modern Mathematics

Srinivasa Ramanujan stands as one of the most enigmatic and brilliant figures in the history of mathematics. Born into a poor Brahmin family in Erode, Tamil Nadu, in 1887, Ramanujan’s life was tragically short but profoundly impactful. Operating largely outside the formal academic establishment, he produced thousands of mathematical theorems, equations, and identities, relying on a profound intuition that continues to baffle and inspire mathematicians over a century later.
A Self-Taught Prodigy

Ramanujan’s mathematical journey was almost entirely self-directed. His absolute obsession with numbers began in his early teens when he borrowed a copy of G.S. Carr’s A Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics. This book, essentially a collection of thousands of theorems without proofs, became his mathematical bible.

Because of his singular devotion to mathematics, Ramanujan neglected all other subjects. He lost his university scholarship after failing his non-mathematical coursework and spent years living in poverty, conducting independent research. He recorded his findings in notebooks, writing on slates to save paper, compiling a dense, personal archive of mathematical discoveries. He often credited his insights to the family goddess, Namagiri Thayar, claiming that she revealed complex mathematical formulas to him in his dreams.
The Letter to Cambridge

The turning point in Ramanujan’s life occurred in 1913. Working as a humble clerk at the Madras Port Trust to support his family, he began sending letters containing his mathematical theorems to prominent British mathematicians. Most ignored him, viewing his unorthodox, proof-less equations as the work of a crank.

However, his letter to G.H. Hardy, a renowned mathematician at the University of Cambridge, changed the course of mathematical history. Hardy was initially skeptical of the wild, unproven formulas—some of which he recognized, and others that seemed entirely impossible. Upon closer inspection, Hardy realized that the equations "must be true because, if they were not true, no one would have had the imagination to invent them." Recognizing Ramanujan's raw, unparalleled genius, Hardy arranged for him to travel to England.
The Cambridge Years and Collaboration

Arriving at Cambridge in 1914, Ramanujan began a historic collaboration with Hardy. They were an ultimate study in contrasts: Hardy was a rigid, formal mathematician who demanded rigorous, step-by-step proofs, while Ramanujan was an intuitive thinker who arrived at conclusions instantly, often unable to explain the logical steps that got him there.

Despite their differences, the partnership was incredibly fruitful. Together, they made groundbreaking contributions to mathematical analysis, number theory, infinite series, and continued fractions. One of their most famous joint achievements was the Hardy-Ramanujan asymptotic formula, which accurately calculates the number of partitions of an integer—a problem previously thought impossible to solve with such precision.
Illness and Enduring Legacy

The cold English climate, strict vegetarian dietary restrictions, and the stress of his relentless work took a severe toll on Ramanujan’s health. He was diagnosed with tuberculosis and a severe vitamin deficiency. In 1919, deeply ill, he returned to India, where he passed away the following year at the age of just 32.

Ramanujan left behind three notebooks and a "lost notebook" (discovered in 1976) filled with thousands of unpublished, unproven theorems. Mathematicians have spent the last century proving his claims, almost all of which have turned out to be correct. Today, his work has applications that go far beyond what he could have imagined during his lifetime, influencing fields as diverse as computer science, crystallography, and the physics of black holes.

Srinivasa Ramanujan is remembered not just for the formulas he left behind, but for demonstrating the sheer, boundless power of human intuition. He remains the ultimate symbol of raw, untutored genius in the modern scientific era.
